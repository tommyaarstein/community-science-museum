Our Exhibitions

Our museum is filled with all kinds of activities and curiosities

Anatomy

Look at different parts of the human anatomy to learn how it works and how it is built!

Did you know that the heart is a muscular powerhouse?

To give kids an idea of how hard it works, have them grab a tennis ball. To mimic the force of a single heartbeat, you have to squeeze a tennis ball tightly with one hand. Now, imagine doing that 100,000 times a day!

Our blood vessels are incredibly long!

If you could uncoil all the blood vessels that the heart pumps blood through and lay them end-to-end, they would stretch for about 96,560 km.

The secret about the heart sound.

Most people think the "lub-dub" sound of a heartbeat is the muscle squeezing.

Fossils!

Our museum has different fossils of extinct animals from different ages in our earth's history!

The "Fat Hump" Mystery

If you look at a Woolly Mammoth skeleton, you'll notice a very high, pointed ridge on its shoulders. For a long time, people thought this was just a weirdly shaped bone. However, scientists discovered that this skeletal "peak" was actually an anchor for a giant hump of fat, similar to a camel's hump!

Why was it there?

The mammoth used this hump like a built-in battery pack. During the freezing Ice Age winters when grass was buried under snow, the mammoth would "eat" its own shoulder fat to stay alive.

The Look

Even though the skeleton looks bony and sharp at the top, the living animal would have looked much more rounded and "hunchbacked" because of this massive energy reserve.

Insects

Marvel at the fascinating creatures that live hidden in our world.

Ants! The World's Strongest Weightlifters

An ant is like a tiny superhero. Depending on the species, an ant can lift 50 times its own body weight. To put that in "human terms" for your visitors: that would be like a second-grader picking up a full-size SUV over their head!

The "Scent Trail" GPS

Ants don't use Google Maps; they use their noses (well, their antennae). When an ant finds food, it leaves a chemical trail called pheromones on the ground. This acts like a "scent highway" that tells all its sisters exactly where the snacks are. If you see a line of ants, they are literally "reading" the floor with their faces.

Ears in Weird Places

Not all insects hear with their heads! A grasshopper has its ears on its belly (the abdomen), while a cricket has its ears on its front legs, just below the knees. Imagine if you had to point your shins toward someone to hear what they were whispering!
